Gertrude B. Elion

The lady who changed the world of Medicine




Gertrude Belle Elion (January 23, 1918 â€“ February 21, 1999) was an American biochemist and Pharmacologist, who shared the 1988 Nobel prize in Physiology or Medicine with George H. Hitchings and Sir James Black. Working alone as well as with Hitchings and Black, Elion developed a multitude of new drugs, using innovative research methods that would later lead to the development of the AIDS drug AZT.


Career Milestones:

1937: Graduated from Hunter College with a degree in Chemistry

1941: Graduated from New York University (M.SC) while working as a high school teacher during day time.

1967: From 1967 to 1983, she was the Head of the Department of Experimental Therapy for Burroughs Wellcome (now GlaxoSmithKline) and officially retired in 1983. Despite her retirement, Elion continued working almost full-time at the lab, and oversaw the adaptation of azidothymidine (AZT), which became the first drug used for treatment of AIDS

1968: Received Garvan-Olin Medal

1970: Moved to the Research Triangle

1971: Affiliated with Duke University as Adjunct Professor of Pharmacology and of Experimental Medicine from 1971 to 1983

1983: Research Professor in Duke University from 1983 to 1999

1988: Received the Nobel Prize in Medicine, together with Hitchings and Sir James Black

1989: Awarded an honorary Ph.D from New York University Tandon School of Engineering (then Polytechnic University of New York). She never obtained a formal Ph.D.

1990: Elected as a member of the National Academy of Sciences

1991: Elected as a member of the Institute of Medicine

1991: Elected as a Fellow of the American Academy of Arts and Sciences

1991: Received National Medal of Science

1991: She became the first woman to be inducted into the National Inventors Hall of Fame

1995: Elected a Foreign Member of the Royal Society (ForMemRS)

1997: Received Lemelson-MIT Lifetime Achievement Award

1998: Received honorary SD degree from Harvard university
